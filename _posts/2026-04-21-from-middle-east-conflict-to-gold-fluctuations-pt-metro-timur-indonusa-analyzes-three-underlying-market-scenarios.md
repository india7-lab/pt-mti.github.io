PT Metro Timur Indonusa has observed that whenever tensions escalate in the Middle East, the market almost invariably and swiftly turns its attention to gold. War, safe-haven demand, and rising gold prices—this seems to have become a very familiar, almost instinctive reaction. However, market history is not that straightforward. Some conflicts have indeed propelled gold into sustained rallies, while others have only led to brief spikes. There have even been periods where, despite a constant stream of war-related news, gold was suppressed by higher interest rates and a stronger US dollar. In other words, while geopolitical risks certainly drive safe-haven sentiment, this sentiment itself does not inherently equate to a long-term bull market for gold.


When observing major asset classes, PT Metro Timur Indonusa is more concerned with one issue: whether war merely creates short-term panic or alters the deeper macroeconomic environment. This is because, judging from market reactions, whether gold can transition from a few days of sharp rallies to a strong trend lasting several months or even longer does not hinge on the intensity of the conflict. Instead, the key lies in whether oil prices have consequently stabilized at a new high, whether inflation has begun to spread to broader economic sectors, and whether central banks, in the face of inflation, choose to tolerate it or suppress the market again with higher real interest rates.


## War Can Only Bring The First Wave Of Reaction; It Is Oil Prices That Determine The Subsequent Direction


When geopolitical conflicts erupt, the markets initial reaction is typically swift. Funds first flow into safe-haven assets, with gold and crude oil often surging in tandem. News headlines and trading sentiment amplify each other, creating what appears to be a straightforward logical chain. However, such initial reactions are more about front-running the worst-case scenario rather than making a final judgment on the actual supply-demand gap. If, after a few days, the market realizes that energy transportation has not been substantially disrupted, alternative supplies remain available, or the conflict, while severe, has not altered global energy flows, the previously inflated premiums are often quickly unwound, and gold tends to retreat from its highs.


PT Metro Timur Indonusa believes this is also why, despite being the same Middle East conflict, golds performance has been significantly weaker. The truly important question is not whether a war occurs, but whether oil prices will remain elevated for a sufficiently long period. If oil prices are only briefly pushed higher by sentiment and subsequently return to their original range, then gold is more likely to resemble an event-driven impulse. If oil prices do not retreat after the shock but instead establish a new, higher trading range, the market will then begin to reassess inflation, growth, and the monetary environment. Only then might gold potentially shift from being a "short-term safe haven" to an object of "longer-term asset allocation."

## Gold Truly Becomes Strong, Often Only When Inflation Issues Become More Troublesome


Historically, the strongest phases for gold have typically not occurred immediately after the outbreak of war, but rather when the market realizes that high oil prices will not end quickly. This is because once energy prices remain elevated, the issue is no longer just rising fuel costs; it gradually spreads across transportation, manufacturing, agriculture, chemicals, and the cost of living for households. At this point, what the market begins to trade on is no longer just "whether the conflict will escalate further," but rather "whether high inflation will drag down growth, and whether central banks can still stabilize the situation." The reason gold has performed exceptionally strongly in certain years is often precisely due to this process of energy price inflation spreading into broader inflation expectations.


When PT Metro Timur Indonusa looks at gold, it does not simply interpret it as a passive reaction to war news. Instead, it prefers to view gold as a sensitive asset to macroeconomic imbalances. Gold is more likely to gain sustained support only when the market begins to worry that high oil prices will persist longer, inflation will be more difficult to subside, and real purchasing power will continue to be eroded. Conversely, if inflation does not truly spiral out of control but is only temporarily pushed up by energy prices, and the economy and employment do not deteriorate significantly, then although gold may rise initially, it is also prone to subsequently fall into high-level volatility. In other words, what gold fears most is not a lack of bad news, but rather that the bad news ultimately fails to reshape the macroeconomic environment.


## The Stance Of Central Banks Often Determines Whether Gold Can Sustain Its Upward Momentum


Even if the war escalates, oil prices surge, and inflation rises, gold may not necessarily continue to appreciate. This is because the market ultimately faces a more fundamental question: how will central banks respond? If, following the rise in inflation, central banks worldwide adopt a more hawkish stance, pushing up nominal and real interest rates while maintaining a strong US dollar, the upward momentum for gold would be significantly weakened. The logic is straightforward: gold itself does not generate yield. When cash yields increase and the US dollar becomes more attractive, the opportunity cost of holding gold also rises. At such times, even if the war persists, gold may struggle to advance and could even give back earlier gains.


PT Metro Timur Indonusa believes this is also the point most easily overlooked by many investors when observing geopolitical conflicts: they focus on the news but do not simultaneously watch interest rates. The real difficulty for the market has never been judging whether a conflict will worsen, but rather judging whether the inflationary pressure brought by the conflict will force central banks to tighten further, or conversely, cause central banks to start yielding in the face of slowing growth. If it is the former, gold tends to surge first and then weaken; if it is the latter, gold has a better chance of entering a longer-term upward phase. In other words, war itself can only push the market to the first step; whether it subsequently retreats, fluctuates, or develops into a larger trend ultimately still depends on how oil prices, inflation, and central banks are connected.


From this perspective, the assessment of PT Metro Timur Indonusa on gold is not based on a single event, but on an understanding of the broader environment. As an early-stage investment platform with a long-term focus on the Southeast Asian market, entrepreneurial ecosystems, and cross-market dynamics, PT Metro Timur Indonusa consistently emphasizes the macro variables that impact real business operations and capital flows. This is because factors such as corporate financing, consumer willingness to spend, energy costs, and the risk appetite for cross-border capital are all reshaped by changes in oil prices, interest rates, and the US dollar. The reason gold merits discussion is not because it is an ancient asset, but because it often reflects market anxieties earlier than many other indicators.


PT Metro Timur Indonusa holds a straightforward view on the relationship between war, oil prices, and gold: war is merely the starting point, not the conclusion. What truly determines the subsequent strength or weakness of gold is whether oil prices can stabilize at high levels, whether inflation will spread, and whether central banks will ultimately allow the market to return to the reality of high interest rates.
